How can I use the orientation tool?

How can I use the search engine? This engine gives you several different search options:
  • To find an office or a classroom: select "a place" and type in the number of the room, with or without spaces. (e.g. you know that the Computer Science and Communications Faculty occupies room INM 168. Type "inm168" into the search engine, and you will unearth the department's administration reception.)
  • To locate a person: you can do this by writing in his name (even part of his name) or his CAMIPRO (or SCIPER) number, and choosing "a person"; if there are several people with the same name, a list will appear below the plan.
  • To find a service by its usual or by its generic name: we have set up a system for searching by "alias". In this way, certain common or generic terms can be used to locate a service. For example: "restaurant", "library", "money", "computers", "mail", "transport", etc.
  • To find a unit: type in the acronym of the unit you are looking for, choose the "a place" option. The map will show you a list of rooms normally occupied by this unit.
CAUTION: the "search by unit" function is still is its test phase!

The search is based on the directory, for the moment, that is to say, we show rooms occupied by one or more members of the particular unit in question. Therefore only occupied rooms will be included so far!
If you have any questions about this, speak to the various people responsible for these room allocations in the faculties..
The name appearing on the door of my office has nothing to do with the name in the orientation tool. Why is that? New room naming rules were adopted during the year 2002. These rules were chosen on the basis of the "physical" names appearing on the doors. This system is older, and there is little doubt that we are going to have to wait some time before there is an exact correspondence for all 10,000 rooms in the school. Meanwhile, here is a simple explanation of the rules chosen:

The new naming rules may only contain 4 elements (BZLR) the meaning of which are as follows
  • B for Building (e.g. MA for the Mathematics Building)
  • Z for Zone (e.g. CH A for zone A in the Chemistry Building)
  • L for Level
  • R for Room.

The rule as applied is a mix of these elements, always in the order given above. In principal, at least the Building and the room number are included (BR rule) so that the abbreviation for the space is sufficiently explicit.

A building where the BZLR rule applies will therefore have its rooms "numbered" in the following way: Building, followed by Zone, followed by Level, followed by Room number. While the rooms in a building using the BR rule will simply be coded with the Building, followed by the Room number.

In practice, the most frequently used rule is BR (currently 56 buildings [as at March 2003]). Then come the BLR rule (15 buildings) and the BZLR rule (11 buildings). The BZR rule is currently only used in the PA building.

Any question on this topic can be addressed to Roland Fazan, in the Operations and Construction Department.
Some places may be searched via a normal or generic term (e.g. "restaurants"). How does that work? We have the capacity to generate and record specific "views" and to associate them with one or more "aliases", normal or generic terms.

For example, the "Agepoly" can be found by typing in "agep", "agepoly", "student association", "students" or even "student" (always without forgetting to select the "a place" option). This search calls up view number 75. On its web site, the Agepoly could therefore insert a link like this: http://plan.epfl.ch/?view=75
